VibeDispatcher vs. Claude Code Jarvis V

Two voice tools you've built, both sitting in your menu bar, but they help you in different ways. Here's what each does, where they overlap, and the moves that would make them both better with little extra work.

VibeDispatcher — your voice command center for VS Code

Sits in your menu bar. Listens for your voice while you're coding in VS Code. Speaks alerts at you when something needs attention. Comes with a phone app so you can see what your agents are doing when you're away from your Mac, and get push notifications when something can't wait.

Claude Code Jarvis V — your voice for Claude Code (the terminal)

Sits in your menu bar. Lets you have a back-and-forth voice conversation with Claude Code, including with the actual JARVIS voice from Iron Man. Shows you a live transcript of what was said. Lets you switch voice characters from a small floating panel without leaving what you're doing.

VibeDispatcher only Jarvis V only Both do this — different code, same job

Do this firstQuick wins (about a day each)

Take the two best pieces of code Jarvis V has — the safe key storage and the voice-typo filter — and let VibeDispatcher use them too. Result: you fix a known security weakness in VibeDispatcher AND make both apps better at handling Whisper's mistakes, with one piece of work covering both.

Do this soonPhone app shows Jarvis V chats (1-2 days)

Hook Jarvis V's transcript stream up to VibeDispatcher's existing phone app. You get to read what Claude said in your voice conversations from your phone, while you're away from the Mac. No new server. No new app to install. Just plumbing between two things you already own.

Do this when readyBigger build — share the voice library + visual style (1-2 weeks)

Right now both apps maintain their own separate list of voices. Pull them into one shared library. Add a new voice once, both apps see it. Same with the visual style — share the floating widget design and the brand colors so when you ship a third menu-bar tool, it inherits the look automatically.

Don't merge them into one product. They live on different surfaces (VS Code vs. the terminal) and serve different needs (alert triage vs. character-driven voice). The win is shared parts + cross-publishing, not consolidation.
